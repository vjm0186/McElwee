Filing Will


A Will is a document which disposes of a person's property after his/her death, 
it generally names a person's Executive or Representative or Administrator 
of the estate.

When to file a Will:

The custodian of the deceased's original Will must deposit the Will with the 
Circuit Court in the County where the decedent was domiciled within 10 days 
after learning of the person is deceased.

The custodian must supply the person's Death Certificate with a person's 
Social Security number to the clerk upon deposit the original Will.

You do not need an attorney to file the original Will with the Clerk of the 
Circuit Court.  However you may want to consult with an attorney before filing 
the original Will. The attorney should be able to tell whether or not Probate 
is necessary based upon the wording of the Will and the Trust.

Probate is necessary only when things are unclear as to who the Executive is
and how to make distributions.

Probate


      Probate is a legal process through which the
      assets of a deceased person are properly
      distributed to the heirs or benefciaries under a
      Will or, if there is no Will, according to Florida law.
      The court oversees the estate to make sure debts
      are paid and proper distribution is made. 
            
      If it is unclear about the distribution of the estate and
      debts and who is the Executive/Representative then a 
      Probate is necessary.
            
      There is a lot of paperwork involved with a Probate.
      Newspapers must have publishings, Mail must be 
      registered, documents must be proved and finalized by
      a judge. It could take years to complete. Also note 
      that Probate is open for public viewing.
            
      The Last Will and Testament and Trust should avoid a 
      Probate since it names the Executive/Representative 
      and where the distributions go.
            
      The Revocable Living Trust is a Pour-Over from the 
      Last Will and Testament, it is a specific type of 
      legal document used in conjunction with the Will.  
      Think of it as a safety net. Its primary job is to 
      "catch" any assets you owned in your individual 
      name at the time of your death and "pour" them 
      into your Trust so they can be distributed 
      according to the Trust's instructions.
