Directing that a property be sold in a Florida will rather than being "bequeathed" (given directly) to the offspring is a common strategy, but it has specific legal and financial consequences in Florida—particularly regarding the state's unique Homestead Laws.

Here is how this decision breaks down:

1. Consequences: Sale vs. Bequest
The main consequence of "directing a sale" in the will is that it changes how the property is treated by the court.

Loss of Creditor Protection (Homestead): In Florida, a primary residence (Homestead) is normally protected from the deceased person's creditors. However, if the will mandates that the property be sold and the cash split among the offspring, the property may lose its "protected" status. This means creditors (like credit card companies or medical billers) could potentially take the sale proceeds before your offspring see a dime.

Power of Sale: If the will specifically grants the Personal Representative (Executor) the "Power of Sale," they can sell the property without asking the judge for a specific order each time. This makes the process faster and cheaper.

Avoiding "Co-Ownership" Drama: If you bequeath the house to three children, they all own it together. If they can’t agree on whether to keep it or sell it, they might end up in a "Partition Lawsuit," which is expensive. Directing a sale avoids this by having the estate handle the transaction and hand out cash instead.

2. Is there a set time limit to sell?
Florida law does not set a strict "deadline" (e.g., 90 days) to sell the house, but there are practical and legal "clocks":

The "Timely Manner" Rule: Courts expect the estate to be settled in a "timely manner," generally within 12 months for a formal administration.

Creditor Period: A sale often doesn't close until the 90-day creditor claim period has passed. This ensures the title is clear and no surprise bills are attached to the person who died.

Tax Motivation: To take full advantage of the "stepped-up basis" (which minimizes capital gains tax), it is usually best to sell the property relatively soon after the death.

3. Expenses the offspring will face
If the estate is selling the property, the "offspring" don't usually pay out of pocket; instead, these expenses are deducted from their eventual inheritance:

Closing Costs: Standard Florida real estate costs (typically 6% to 9% of the sale price), including realtor commissions and "Doc Stamps" (transfer taxes).

Carrying Costs: Until the house sells, the estate must pay for homeowners insurance, property taxes, utilities, and lawn maintenance.

Legal/Probate Fees: The attorney and Personal Representative are usually paid a percentage of the estate's value (often around 3% each for the first $1M).

Repairs: If the house needs a new roof or AC to sell, that money comes out of the estate funds.

4. Will Florida probate laws rule?
Yes. Unless the property is in a Living Trust or has a Lady Bird Deed, it must go through probate to be sold.

Court Oversight: Even if the will says "sell it," the court must officially appoint the Personal Representative (via "Letters of Administration") before they have the legal authority to sign a deed for the buyer.

Homestead Determination: If it was the parent's primary residence, the attorney will likely file a "Petition to Determine Homestead." This confirms that the property belongs to the heirs (or the sale proceeds do) and is protected from certain debts.
